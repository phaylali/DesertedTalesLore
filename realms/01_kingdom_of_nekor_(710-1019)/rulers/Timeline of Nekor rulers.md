## Notes

Salih I was the founder of the Kingdom of Nekor. In 712, the local Berber tribes, finding the restrictions of Islam too difficult, deposed him and installed Dawud al-Rundi al-Nafzi. However, after a few months, they were dissatisfied with the new ruler and reinstated Salih I, who then ruled until his death in 749. capital was temsaman.
Al-Mu'tasim ibn Salih was known for his piety.
Idris I ibn Salih founded the city of Nekor, which later became the capital.
Sa'id I ibn Idris moved the capital from Temsaman to Nekor.
Salih II ibn Sa'id faced a revolt from his own brother.
Sai'd II Was ultimately defeated and killed by the Fatimid general Messala ibn Habus.
Salih III Acknowledged the Umayyads as rightful caliphs after his family regained power.
Abd al-Badi' ibn Salih Was defeated and killed by Musa ibn Abi'l-Afiya, who destroyed Nekor.
Abu Ayyub Isma'il ibn 'Abd al Malik Was defeated and killed by the Fatimid general Sandal.
Musa ibn Rumi Defeated the governor left by Sandal.
Abd as-Sami' ibn Jurthum Was killed by his own people.
Jurthum ibn Ahmad Adopted the Maliki school of Islamic law and was the last ruler of the dynasty before its end in 1019 CE.
A long-distance Viking expedition entered the Mediterranean and launched a major raid on Nekor in 859 CE. The Viking fleet, reportedly 62 ships strong, defeated a local force and plundered the city, taking many inhabitants as slaves. Some of these captives were later ransomed by the Umayyad Caliph at CÃ³rdoba. This event demonstrates that the Emirate of Nekor, despite its small size, was not an isolated entity but was deeply integrated into the wider Mediterranean world. The Viking raid highlights its vulnerability as a coastal state and confirms its strategic importance to the larger Islamic powers, whose involvement in ransoming captives further solidifies the vassal relationship.
