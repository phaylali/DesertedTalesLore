Tarif al-Matghari
Tarif al-Matghari (died c. 744) was a Berber leader of the Barghawata tribal confederation who founded an independent dynasty in the Tamesna region of present-day Morocco amid the aftermath of the Great Berber Revolt against Umayyad rule.[1]
Succeeding figures in his lineage, notably his son Salih ibn Tarif, who declared himself a prophet around 744 and authored a Berber-language scripture blending Islamic, Jewish, and indigenous elements, extended the kingdom's resistance to orthodox Islamic authority for over 300 years until its conquest by the Almoravids in 1058.[1][2]
The Barghawata state under Tarif al-Matghari's foundational influence maintained agricultural prosperity, a distinct religious practice—including ritual tattooing, veneration of local saints, and rejection of Arabic prayer formulas—and military autonomy, defying caliphal integration efforts despite external Arab chroniclers' portrayals of their doctrines as heretical distortions.[1]
This era exemplified Berber assertions of cultural and political sovereignty in the Maghreb, with the dynasty's longevity underscoring effective adaptation to regional threats, though its syncretism invited conquest narratives emphasizing religious deviation over indigenous resilience.[2]
Early Life and Origins
Background and Tribal Affiliations
Little is known of Tarif al-Matghari's early life. His family originated from indigenous Berber tribes, which were undergoing profound disruptions from Arab-Islamic conquests and expansions during the Umayyad era, including taxation impositions and social hierarchies favoring Arab elites.[3] These tribes maintained distinct linguistic, social, and customary structures rooted in pre-Islamic North African traditions, often centered on clan-based loyalties and pastoral or agricultural economies in the Maghreb's coastal plains.[3] Tarif's primary tribal affiliation linked him to the proto-Barghawata groups, a Berber confederation emerging in the Atlantic coastal zone of Tamesna amid resistance to Umayyad rule.[1] The Barghawata, named possibly after a phonetic adaptation of "Barbati" or tied to local Amazigh (Berber) lineages, represented a coalition of tribes that coalesced during the Great Berber Revolt of 739–743 CE, led initially by figures like Maysara al-Matghari, suggesting shared clan ties through the al-Matghari nisba.[1] [4] This confederation drew from Berber populations disillusioned with perceived inequities in Islamic governance, prioritizing autonomy over centralized caliphal authority.[1] Historical accounts, preserved largely through Arab chroniclers, portray such affiliations as fluid alliances rather than rigid ethnic monoliths, shaped by geographic isolation and anti-Arab sentiments.[2]
Possible Connections to Al-Andalus
Some hypotheses link Tarif al-Matghari's tribal affiliations to Al-Andalus through the etymology of "Barghawata," proposing derivation from the Rio Barbate (ancient Barbat) near Cádiz in southern Spain, a region settled by Berber contingents during the 711 conquest. This suggests possible origins among Berber migrants or veterans who received land grants in Al-Andalus before returning to or settling in the Tamesna region of Morocco.[1][2] One account posits that Tarif himself earned such a grant near Rio de Barbat for participation in jihad against Visigothic forces, potentially explaining the tribal name's persistence among his descendants in the Maghreb. This view holds that many historians trace Barghawata identity to this Spanish locale, contrasting with traditions portraying them as indigenous Masmuda Berbers long established in Doukkala-Abda. However, medieval Arabic sources like those of al-Ya'qubi (d. 897) and Ibn Khaldun (d. 1406) emphasize local Berber roots without explicit ties to Iberian expeditions, rendering the connection speculative and reliant on later interpretations rather than contemporary records.[1]
Role in Berber Resistance
Participation in Anti-Umayyad Revolts
Tarif al-Matghari, a Berber chieftain of the Matghara tribe, actively participated in the Great Berber Revolt against Umayyad authority from 740 to 743 AD, amid widespread discontent over Arab-imposed taxation, second-class status for converts, and military conscription.[1] The uprising, fueled by Sufri Kharijite doctrines emphasizing egalitarian piety over ethnic hierarchy, saw Berber forces initially overwhelm Umayyad garrisons in Tangier and spread southward into the Maghreb.[2] As a tribal leader in the Tamesna region, Tarif aligned with rebel contingents challenging Damascus's control, contributing to the fragmentation of Umayyad power in Morocco despite eventual counteroffensives by Umayyad governors such as Kulthum ibn Iyad.[1] Historical accounts portray Tarif's involvement as peripheral to the core leadership under Maysara al-Matghari, who ignited the revolt by slaying the governor of Tangier in 740 AD before his own execution by mutinous followers later that year.[5] Yet, the revolt's decentralized nature—marked by tribal alliances and ideological fervor—enabled figures like Tarif to consolidate local resistance, evading full Umayyad reconquest and laying groundwork for autonomous Berber polities. By 743 AD, as Umayyad reinforcements under Kulthum ibn Iyad quelled main Kharijite strongholds, Tarif's survival and relocation to Tamesna exemplified the revolt's partial success in birthing enduring separatist entities.[1]
Association with Maysara al-Matghari
Tarif al-Matghari participated in the Great Berber Revolt of 740–743, which was initially led by Maysara al-Matghari, a Sufri Kharijite from the same tribal background indicated by their shared nisba al-Matghari.[1][5] The revolt erupted in Tangier on 17 June 740, sparked by Berber grievances against Umayyad Arab governors' discriminatory taxation, enslavement practices, and assertions of ethnic superiority, with Maysara mobilizing forces that rapidly overran key cities like Ceuta and advanced into the interior.[6][7] Tarif's involvement aligned with the broader anti-Umayyad coalition of Berber tribes, including the Barghawata confederation to which he belonged, contributing to the rebels' control over much of present-day Morocco by late 740.[1] Maysara proclaimed himself caliph and enforced Kharijite egalitarianism, but internal strife led to his murder by mutinous troops in early 741 near the Sus valley, fragmenting the movement as leadership shifted to figures like Khawlan ibn Yazid al-Hasamy.[5][6] In the revolt's aftermath, Tarif disengaged from the central campaigns around 742–744, withdrawing to the Tamesna plain along Morocco's Atlantic coast to consolidate Barghawata autonomy rather than pursue the failing broader insurgency, which Umayyad reinforcements under Kulthum ibn Iyad ultimately suppressed by 743.[7][5] This strategic divergence marked Tarif's transition from revolt participant to independent ruler, establishing a polity that endured Umayyad reconquests through localized resistance and later religious innovation under his successors.[1]
Founding of the Barghawata Dynasty
Establishment in Tamesna Region
Tarif al-Matghari, a Berber leader from the Matghara subtribe within the larger Barghawata confederation, consolidated authority in the Tamesna region following the collapse of the Great Berber Revolt against Umayyad overlords in 743.[1] This coastal plain along Morocco's Atlantic seaboard, extending roughly between present-day Safi and Salé, provided strategic advantages including fertile agricultural lands, access to maritime trade routes, and natural defenses against incursions from the east.[1][8] In 744, Tarif formalized the Barghawata's independence by ending formal hostilities with the Umayyads, thereby establishing the dynasty's foundational state in Tamesna as a semi-autonomous Berber polity.[8] This establishment capitalized on the power vacuum left by the revolt's suppression, enabling Tarif to unite disparate Masmuda Berber tribes under a centralized leadership structure resistant to Arab-Islamic administrative impositions.[1] Early organization emphasized tribal alliances and localized governance, with Tamesna serving as the core territorial base for subsequent expansions and defenses.[1] Historical accounts, primarily drawn from medieval Arabic chroniclers like Ibn Khaldun, portray Tarif's rule as pragmatic, focusing on stabilization rather than immediate religious innovation, which emerged later under his successors.[2] These sources, while valuable for chronology, reflect orthodox Islamic biases against Berber autonomy movements, potentially understating the indigenous causal factors of tribal self-determination in Tamesna's establishment.[1]
Initial Political and Military Organization
Tarif al-Matghari established the Barghawata polity in the Tamesna region around 744 CE by consolidating disparate Berber tribes into a unified confederacy, securing key fortresses and their surrounding territories to secure a territorial base independent of Umayyad oversight.[8] This organizational shift followed the Barghawata's participation in the broader Berber Revolt (739–743 CE), during which Tarif, initially aligned with rebel forces under Maysara al-Matghari, leveraged tribal alliances to transition from insurgency to state-like autonomy, formally ending hostilities with Arab authorities.[8] The political framework emphasized centralized leadership under Tarif as the founding emir, fostering tribal cohesion through a shared identity that prioritized internal stability over expansion, thereby enabling defensive consolidation rather than offensive campaigns.[8] Militarily, the initial structure relied on tribal levies organized for guerrilla warfare, drawing on Berber traditions of mobility and terrain familiarity in the Atlantic coastal plains, supplemented by a core force dedicated to fortress defense and protection of trade networks.[8] This approach proved effective in repelling residual Umayyad incursions, with the confederacy's forces numbering in the thousands—though exact figures remain unrecorded—focused on tactical ambushes and fortified perimeters rather than open-field battles.[8] Politically, Tarif's regime marked a departure from Sufri Kharijite egalitarianism by institutionalizing hierarchical rule, subordinating doctrinal purity to pragmatic governance that integrated Islamic elements with Berber customs, setting the stage for his successor's prophetic claims.[8]
Family and Immediate Successors
Key Relatives and Lineage
Tarif al-Matghari belonged to the Matghara, a Berber tribe involved in early anti-Umayyad revolts in the Maghreb during the 740s.[1] His most prominent relative was his son, Salih ibn Tarif, who ascended to leadership of the Barghawata confederation following Tarif's death in 744 and proclaimed himself a prophet, diverging from orthodox Islam by asserting a Berber-specific scripture and practices.[5] The Barghawata dynasty's lineage traced patrilineally from Tarif through Salih, with subsequent rulers including Salih's son Ilyas ibn Salih (r. circa 792–842), who consolidated tribal authority; Ilyas's son Yunus (r. 842–888); and Yunus's successors such as Abu Ghufail (r. 888–913), extending familial control over the Tamesna region for generations.[1] [5] This succession emphasized dynastic continuity amid religious innovation, though primary sources on earlier ancestors or siblings of Tarif remain scarce, limited to tribal affiliations rather than individualized kin records.[1] No direct evidence identifies Tarif's father or other immediate relatives beyond the Matghara tribal context, which linked him to figures like Maysara al-Matghari, a revolt leader of the same tribe whose role preceded Tarif's but without confirmed blood ties.[5]
Salih ibn Tarif's Ascension and Prophetic Claims
Salih ibn Tarif, a Berber chieftain of the Banu Barghawata tribe, ascended to leadership around 744 CE following the death of his father Tarif al-Matghari in the aftermath of the Great Berber Revolt against Umayyad rule. Historical accounts indicate that Salih consolidated power in the Tamesna region by leveraging tribal alliances and anti-Arab sentiments, positioning himself as a successor amid the fragmentation of rebel forces. His rise was marked by a strategic shift from mere insurgency to establishing a semi-independent polity, which he framed through religious innovation to legitimize authority. Central to Salih's ascension were his prophetic claims, which he proclaimed to elevate his status beyond a tribal leader to a divinely inspired figure. He asserted revelation from God, declaring himself a prophet and introducing a distinct scripture known as the "Quran of the Barghawata," which reportedly comprised 80 surahs and incorporated Berber linguistic elements alongside Islamic motifs. These claims diverged sharply from orthodox Islam, including assertions that Salih received divine endorsement to adapt religious practices, such as permitting pork consumption and Friday prayers at dawn, which served to differentiate his followers from Arab-dominated Muslim communities. Chroniclers like Ibn Khaldun later described these innovations as heretical, attributing them to Salih's aim to foster Berber cultural autonomy rather than genuine prophecy. Salih's prophetic narrative included messianic elements, with traditions recording that he claimed descent from the prophet Idris (Enoch) and foretold a role in restoring Berber primacy. This self-proclaimed status enabled him to mobilize followers by blending local animist traditions with Islamic terminology, creating a syncretic faith that justified resistance to Umayyad caliphal authority. While some medieval Arab sources, such as al-Bakri, portray these claims as opportunistic fabrications to consolidate power post-revolt, they underscore Salih's success in sustaining the Barghawata polity for over three centuries until its conquest by the Almoravids in 1058 CE. Skepticism regarding the authenticity of his revelations persists in historical analysis, given the absence of contemporaneous non-Berber corroboration and the propagandistic nature of surviving accounts.
Death and Immediate Aftermath
Circumstances of Death in 744
Tarif al-Matghari's death occurred in 744 CE, amid the consolidation of Berber autonomy in the Tamesna region following the Great Berber Revolt of 739–743 CE, during which Arab Umayyad forces faced widespread uprisings across the Maghreb. Historical accounts do not specify the cause, with surviving records—primarily from later Islamic chroniclers—offering no details on violence, illness, or other factors, suggesting a lack of dramatic or contentious elements that might have warranted documentation.[2] [5] This obscurity aligns with the limited preservation of Barghawata-specific sources, as orthodox Muslim historians often marginalized or demonized non-conforming Berber polities, potentially omitting neutral biographical details.[2] The timing places his demise shortly after he relocated to Tamesna post-revolt, where he organized military and political structures among the Barghawata Berbers, distancing from Umayyad reconquest efforts stabilized under Caliph Hisham ibn Abd al-Malik. No evidence indicates death in battle, as Umayyad campaigns had shifted eastward by then, nor assassination by rivals, given the smooth transition to his successor. Instead, it likely reflects natural mortality for a leader active in prior raids and revolts, though his age at death remains unknown due to absent birth records.[5][2] This event prompted immediate dynastic continuity, with power passing to his son Salih ibn Tarif, who intensified religious deviations by self-proclaiming as a prophet. The absence of detailed circumstances underscores source biases in medieval Islamic historiography, which prioritized theological conformity over Berber internal affairs, rendering empirical reconstruction challenging.[2]
Transition of Power
Following Tarif al-Matghari's death in 744, authority over the Barghawata confederacy transferred directly to his son, Ṣāliḥ ibn Tarīf, establishing familial succession as the basis for dynastic continuity in the Tamesna region.[1] This handover occurred amid the aftermath of the Kharijite-influenced revolts against Umayyad rule, with Ṣāliḥ leveraging his father's foundational efforts to maintain Berber autonomy against Arab governors.[1] Ṣāliḥ's ascension marked an immediate ideological evolution, as he proclaimed himself a prophet around 744 or shortly thereafter, authoring a Berber-language scripture divergent from the Quran and instituting rituals blending Islamic, Jewish, and indigenous elements.[5] This prophetic claim, reported in medieval Islamic chronicles, reinforced central authority by framing the Barghawata state as divinely ordained, though it invited condemnation from orthodox sources as heretical innovation. No records indicate significant internal opposition or rival claimants during the transition, suggesting tribal consensus around Ṣāliḥ's lineage and prior involvement in anti-Umayyad activities.[1] The power shift thus transitioned the Barghawata from a revolt-led polity under Tarif—focused on military expulsion of Umayyad forces—to a more structured entity under Ṣāliḥ, with expanded missionary efforts and fortified independence lasting until Almoravid conquests centuries later.[1] Some accounts date Ṣāliḥ's formal rule from 749, potentially reflecting a brief consolidation period post-744, but primary consensus aligns the succession with Tarif's demise.[9]
Legacy and Historical Assessments
Long-Term Impact on Berber Autonomy
The establishment of the Barghawata state under Tarif al-Matghari following the Great Berber Revolt of 739–740 CE enabled a confederation of approximately 40 Berber tribes in the Tamesna region to sustain political independence from Umayyad and subsequent Abbasid authorities for over three centuries, until their conquest by the Almoravids around 1058 CE.[1] This prolonged autonomy stemmed from strategic control over fertile coastal lands, which supported agricultural self-sufficiency and limited external incursions, while the confederacy's military organization, drawing on tribal levies, repelled repeated Arab expeditions.[8] By rejecting orthodox Islamic governance and developing a syncretic religious system—incorporating Berber customs, prophetic claims by leaders like Salih ibn Tarif, and a vernacular scripture in Berber language—the Barghawata fostered cultural resistance to Arabization, preserving indigenous identity and linguistic practices amid broader North African Islamization.[1] This model of localized authority, blending tribal alliances with quasi-theocratic rule, demonstrated viable alternatives to caliphal centralization, influencing the emergence of other Berber-led polities such as the Rostemids in Algeria (777–909 CE) and contributing to the pattern of fragmented, autonomous entities that characterized Maghreb politics through the 11th century.[10] The dynasty's endurance underscored the causal limits of Arab military projection in peripheral regions, where geographic isolation and internal Arab divisions post-750 CE Abbasid revolution allowed Berber groups to negotiate tribute-based peace rather than submit to direct rule, thereby embedding precedents for conditional sovereignty that echoed in later Berber revivals under the Almohads (1121–1269 CE).[1] Although ultimately subdued, the Barghawata's resistance delayed full integration into Sunni orthodoxy, sustaining pockets of Berber customary law and autonomy that persisted in oral traditions and tribal structures, as evidenced by medieval geographers' accounts of their distinct practices.[8]
Evaluations in Islamic and Berber Sources
Islamic historiographers, primarily from Sunni orthodox traditions, evaluated Tarif al-Matghari as the founder of a deviant Berber polity in Tamesna that challenged Umayyad authority post-740 revolt, portraying his leadership as the precursor to outright religious innovation under his successor Salih ibn Tarif. Al-Bakri (d. 1094), in his geographical compendium, details the Barghawata's syncretic practices—including ritual prayers toward the sun, consumption of certain foods forbidden in orthodox Islam, and a purported 80-sura Quran authored by Salih—classifying them as zandaqa (heretical deviation) warranting eradication, evidenced by reports of their fortified resistance and eventual subjugation by Almoravid forces around 1058.[1] Ibn Khaldun (d. 1406), in his Muqaddimah and history of the Berbers, frames Tarif's establishment as a tribal secession exploiting post-revolt chaos, critiquing it as asabiyyah-driven rebellion that blended Kharijite egalitarianism with pagan remnants, ultimately unsustainable against unified caliphal power, though he acknowledges their military tenacity in repelling invasions until the Almoravids.[1] These accounts reflect a consistent orthodox lens emphasizing doctrinal purity, with Tarif's role minimized in favor of Salih's prophetic pretensions, which sources like Ibn Hazm (d. 1064) decry as false prophecy superseding Muhammad's finality, justifying fatwas for jihad against them; however, some Maghribi chronicles preserve ambivalent tones, noting the Barghawata's piety in moral austerity—such as bans on alcohol and usury—suggesting not total infidelity but "devout heresy" that preserved Islamic veneer amid cultural resistance.[11] This nuance arises because early sources, drawing from eyewitness Berber informants, highlight their initial adherence to Sufri Kharijism before Salih's reforms, indicating Tarif's foundational phase as politically autonomous rather than theologically extreme.[12] Berber sources on Tarif are sparse and mediated through oral traditions later transcribed by Islamic or colonial-era ethnographers, lacking independent pre-modern texts; Ibadi chronicles from neighboring Rustamid state (e.g., via al-Wargalani's compilations) view the Barghawata as excessive radicals diverging from moderate Kharijism, criticizing their prophetic claims as bid'ah (innovation) that alienated fellow Berbers, yet implicitly respecting Tarif's role in securing Tamesna autonomy from Arab fiscal exactions post-739.[1] In modern Amazigh revivalist interpretations drawing from these fragments, Tarif symbolizes proto-nationalist defiance, prioritizing Berber matrilineal customs and agrarian self-sufficiency over Arabo-Islamic imposition, though such readings project contemporary identity onto scant historical data without primary Berber attestation.[12] The scarcity underscores reliance on adversarial Islamic narratives, which, while biased toward orthodoxy, provide the verifiable core of Tarif's legacy as a catalyst for localized syncretism enduring over three centuries.
Controversies and Interpretations
Orthodox Islamic Perspectives on Heresy and Rebellion
Orthodox Islamic scholars and historians, particularly from the Abbasid and subsequent eras, classified the Barghawata movement under Tarif al-Matghari and his successors as a form of baghy (rebellion) against legitimate caliphal authority, rooted in the Great Berber Revolt of 740–743 CE, which challenged Umayyad rule over issues of taxation and ethnic discrimination but escalated into separatist autonomy.[1] This perspective holds that, despite initial grievances, the revolt deviated from Islamic jurisprudence permitting uprising only against manifest unbelief (kufr bawah), as Umayyad governance, while flawed, adhered to core shari'a principles; thus, Barghawata resistance constituted illicit sedition warranting suppression.[13] The religious dimensions amplified condemnation, with Salih ibn Tarif's 744 CE proclamation of prophethood—positioning himself as a successor to Muhammad—deemed unequivocal heresy (zandaqa), violating the doctrinal finality of prophethood (khatm al-nubuwwa) affirmed in Quran 33:40 and hadith collections like Sahih al-Bukhari.[14] Orthodox critiques, echoed in works by Ibn Khaldun and al-Bakri, highlighted syncretic corruptions such as a fabricated Berber Quran comprising 80 surahs (versus the orthodox 114), ritual prayers oriented toward the sun rather than Mecca, and a three-month fast diverging from Ramadan, interpreting these as deliberate distortions (tahrif) blending Islamic forms with pre-Islamic Berber paganism and astrology to legitimize independence.[13] Such innovations were seen not as mere cultural adaptation but as bid'ah (heretical innovation) leading to apostasy, justifying jihad against the Barghawata as defensive warfare against internal threats to the umma.[15] Maghribi historiography, including Abbasid-aligned chroniclers, portrayed the Barghawata as "devout heretics"—superficially Islamic yet fundamentally deviant—whose longevity until 1149 CE underscored the perils of unchecked tribalism eroding doctrinal unity, though some accounts note their military resilience stemmed from unified Berber identity rather than theological appeal.[13] This view persists in Sunni orthodoxy, where the movement exemplifies how rebellion fused with prophetic imposture fosters fitna (strife), contrasting with legitimate Berber revolts like those of the Almoravids that realigned with caliphal norms; Arab-centric biases in these sources, prioritizing caliphal legitimacy, may overemphasize heresy to delegitimize Berber autonomy, yet the objective falsehood of post-Muhammadic prophethood remains unassailable per first-principles Islamic theology.[1][14]
Alternative Views on Cultural Syncretism and Independence
Some historians interpret the Barghawata confederation, founded by Tarif al-Matghari following the Great Berber Revolt of 739–740 CE, as an assertion of Berber cultural autonomy through religious adaptation rather than outright rejection of Islam. This perspective frames their syncretic practices—blending Islamic elements with Berber, Jewish, and Christian influences—as a strategic preservation of indigenous identity amid Arab Umayyad dominance, enabling political independence in the Tamesna region until their elimination by the Almohads in 1149 CE.[16] [17][18] In this view, the use of a Tamazight-language Qur'anic interpretation under Salih ibn Tarif's prophetic leadership represented linguistic and doctrinal independence, reconciling Amazigh traditions with Abrahamic faiths without full submission to Arabic orthodoxy. Scholars like John Iskander have characterized this as a potential "Berber national liberation movement" against Arab domination, highlighting how syncretism fostered resilience, with the confederation maintaining 42 tribes and agricultural self-sufficiency for over three centuries.[16] Alternative assessments emphasize causal factors such as pre-Islamic Berber-Jewish alliances and lingering Christian remnants post-conquest, positing syncretism as an organic evolution rather than heresy, which sustained the dynasty's defiance of caliphal authority, including their defeat of the Almoravid leader Abdallah ibn Yasin in 1058 CE, until Almohad conquest in 1149 CE. These interpretations prioritize empirical evidence from Berber oral histories and archaeological sites in Atlantic Morocco, viewing the Barghawata state as a model of cultural hybridity that delayed Arabization in the far Maghrib.[16][17][18]
