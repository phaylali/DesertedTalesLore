# Salih ibn Tarif

## Overview
Salih ibn Tarif (c. 724 – c. 791) was the prophet-king of the Barghawata who transformed his father's tribal confederation into a theocratic state. Author of the **Berber Quran**—an 80-surah scripture in Tamazight—he claimed the prophetic mantle to bring Islam closer to the Amazigh people and solidify their commitment to monotheism through culturally resonant practice, rather than to pursue personal glory or power.

## Origins & Lineage

### Birth & Early Life
Born c. 720 in the newly-founded Barghawata confederation of Tamesna, Salih grew up during his father's consolidation of tribal autonomy following the Great Berber Revolt. Raised in the agricultural settlements of Atlantic Morocco, he witnessed:
- The practical challenges of maintaining independence from Umayyad/Abbasid authority
- The cultural tension between Arab Islamic orthodoxy and Amazigh customary practices
- The limitations of foreign religious authority for indigenous populations

### Family Relations
| Relation | Name | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Father | **Tarif al-Matghari** | Founder; died c. 744; unaware of son's religious intentions |
| Son (& Successor) | **Ilyas ibn Salih** | Consolidated the faith; ruled c. 792–842 |
| Grandson | **Yunus ibn Ilyas** | Third-generation ruler; institutionalized religious practice |

### Cultural Context
Salih's generation faced a critical dilemma: the Berber peoples of Morocco had accepted Islam in principle but faced:
- **Linguistic barriers:** Arabic Quran inaccessible to Tamazight speakers
- **Cultural marginalization:** Arab Caliphate treated Berbers as secondary converts (*mawali*)
- **Political subordination:** Financial and military exploitation by Umayyad governors

## Biography

### The Succession (c. 744)
Upon Tarif's death, Salih inherited leadership of the confederation. His father had established political autonomy; Salih recognized that survival required **cultural and religious autonomy** as well. Tarif had been a pragmatist; Salih would become a visionary.

### The Prophetic Declaration (c. 749)
At approximately age 29, Salih proclaimed himself **Prophet and Mahdi** to the assembled tribes of the confederation. This declaration was not motivated by personal ambition for glory or power, but by a conviction that the Amazigh peoples needed an Islamic practice that resonated with their language, customs, and identity.

#### Theological & Political Context
Salih's claim drew upon **Kharijite doctrinal foundations** established during the 740 revolt:
- **Egalitarianism:** The Kharijite sect held that legitimate Muslim leadership required only piety and capability—not Quraysh/Sharifian lineage
- **Scriptural accessibility:** If any righteous Muslim could lead, then scripture itself should be accessible in the people's tongue
- **Indigenous legitimacy:** A prophet for the Berbers was no more radical than a prophet for the Arabs had been

**Critical Principle:** Salih's position was that while Muhammad remained prophet for the Arabs, the Berbers—equal in spiritual dignity—deserved their own prophetic figure to guide them in their language and cultural context.

### The Berber Quran (c. 749–793)

#### Structural Innovations
| Feature | Orthodox Quran | Salih's Quran | Rationale |
|---------|---------------|---------------|-----------|
| **Surahs** | 114 | **80** | Condensed for oral transmission; thematic clustering |
| **Language** | Classical Arabic | **Tamazight** | Direct accessibility for Amazigh peoples |
| **Opening** | *Al-Fatiha* | **"Ayûb" (Job)** | Emphasizes patience and trial, resonating with Amazigh resistance narratives |
| **God's Name** | *Allah* | **"Yakouch"** | Amazigh theological term; familiar to Berber converts from Christian/Jewish backgrounds |
| **Prophethood** | Muhammad as Seal | **Salih as "Warya Wari"** (He who has no successor) | Final prophet for the Berber peoples, parallel to Muhammad's role for Arabs |

#### Doctrinal Synthesis
The 80-surah text represented syncretism not for novelty, but accessibility:
- **Islamic core:** Monotheism, prayer, ethical conduct
- **Amazigh expression:** 10 daily prayers rather than 5 (shorter, distributed)
- **Agricultural calendar:** Fasting periods aligned with local harvest cycles
- **Inclusive theology:** Jesus (*ʿĪsā*) as companion to Salih; recognition of Hebrew prophets

### Governance Implementation (c. 749–793)

While Tarif founded the political structure, **Salih established the detailed governance**:

| Domain | Tarif's Foundation | Salih's Development |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Political** | Tribal confederation | Theocratic kingship with prophetic legitimacy |
| **Religious** | Sufri Kharijite tolerance | Distinctive Barghawata faith (12 tribes) |
| **Legal** | Customary Berber law | Scriptural basis in Berber Quran |
| **Military** | Defensive guerrilla tactics | Organized 12,000+ cavalry |
| **Cultural** | Agricultural autonomy | Independent script and calendar |

#### The Twelve vs. Seventeen
Of the 29 tribal confederation members:
- **12 tribes** adopted Salih's Berber Quran and practices
- **17 tribes** maintained orthodox or Kharijite Islam

This division was pragmatic—Salih's system proved attractive to those seeking cultural integration; others maintained existing ties to broader Islamic networks.

### The Disappearance (c. 793)
At age 47 (numerologically significant: 7×7−2), Salih "returned to God"—a departure leaving no body. Medieval sources records that he **promised to return** at the consummation of the age, establishing **baraka transmission** through his lineage:
- Only descendants of Salih possessed legitimate **blessed saliva** (*baraka*)
- Succession passed patrilineally: Ilyas → Yunus → subsequent dynasts

**Historical Interpretation:** Likely death natural or through illness, mythologized as cosmic return to maintain authority structure and doctrinal continuity.

## Theological Significance

### The Motivation Question

**Rejected Interpretation:** Salih sought personal power and glory through false prophecy.

**Canonical Interpretation:** Salih sought to **bring Islam closer to the Amazigh people** and solidify their commitment to monotheistic faith. His prophetic claim addressed a specific pastoral concern:
- Arabic Quran remained linguistically inaccessible
- Arab caliphal authority exploited converts rather than welcomed them
- Without indigenous religious leadership, Berber Islam risked dissolving or remaining subordinate

Salih's solution was bold and heterodox: rather than waiting for Arab religious authorities to accommodate Amazigh needs, he provided an indigenous Islamic expression that maintained theological continuity while ensuring 

### Kharijite Egalitarianism

The theological legitimacy of Salih's movement derived from **Kharijite principles**:

**The Sharifian Question:**  
Mainstream Sunni Islam held that legitimate caliphal authority required descent from Quraysh (Muhammad's tribe). This genealogical requirement effectively excluded Berbers from leadership positions.

**Kharijite Position:**  
Leadership required only:
1. Devout practice (*taqwa*)
2. Capability (*kifāya*)
3. Community acceptance (*bayʿa*)

Salih extended this logic: if a Berber could legitimately lead politically (as Tarif had), a Berber could legitimately prophesy religiously—particularly for their own people. The **80-surah Berber Quran** was the concrete expression of this doctrinal egalitarianism.

## Religious Practices

### Prayer
- **Ten sessions daily** (vs. five)
- **Standing for 40 surahs**, **seated for 40 surahs**
- **Orientation:** Toward sunrise (not Mecca)
- **Language:** Tamazight recitation

### Fasting
- **Three-day concentrated fast** (not lunar month)
- Timed to agricultural cycles rather than Hijri calendar

### Dietary Law
- **Permitted:** Pork (prohibited to Arabs but not indigenous)
- **Prohibited:** Eggs, fowl (traditional Amazigh taboos integrated)
- **Wine:** Contextually permitted

### Sacred Transmission
- **Baraka in saliva:** Only descendants of Salih possessed blessed saliva for healing/ritual purposes
- **Matrilineal blessing transmission:** Sacred status passed through women as well as men

## Legacy & Assessment

### Historical Impact
- Established **314-year** indigenous theocratic state (744–1058)
- Created model for **Amazigh Islamic autonomy** distinct from Arab caliphal systems
- Preserved Tamazight language in religious context for three centuries
- Demonstrated viability of non-Arab Islamic practice

### Modern Significance

**Marginalized Legacy:**  
Barghawata rarely appears in Moroccan educational curricula; orthodox Islamic historiography condemned Salih as false prophet or heresiarch.

**Amazigh Reclamation:**  
Modern indigenous rights movements reclaim Salih as:
- Cultural resistance leader
- Linguistic preservation hero
- Proponent of Islamic egalitarianism against Arab ethnic hierarchy
- "Devout heretic" — authentically Muslim despite divergence

**Academic Assessment:**  
Contemporary historians generally acknowledge that Salih's motivations likely combined genuine pastoral concern with political necessity—neither purely cynical power-grab nor purely altruistic ministry, but a synthesis of religious conviction and state-building pragmatism.
