Nekor
Nekor was a historic town and the capital of the Emirate of Nekor, an early independent Muslim state in the Rif region of northern Morocco that existed from 710 to 1019 CE.[1] Founded by the Himyarite Arab chieftain Salih I ibn Mansur under a grant from the Umayyad Caliphate, the emirate was ruled by the Banu Salih dynasty and served as a center for Sunni Maliki Islam in a region influenced by Kharijite and other sects.[2] The town of Nekor itself, initially secondary to the capital at Tamsaman, became prominent under later rulers like Idris I ibn Salih and featured archaeological remains suggesting a mosque, possible public bathhouse, and defensive walls, integrating it into Mediterranean trade networks with al-Andalus and Ifriqiya.[2] The emirate's rulers, descending from Salih I (r. 710–749 CE), included successors like al-Mu'tasim and a series of emirs who often faced internal strife, with many meeting violent ends due to the dynasty's precarious hold.[1] Notable for its autonomy amid the post-Berber Revolt fragmentation of Umayyad authority, Nekor resisted broader caliphal control and fostered conversion of local Berber tribes to Islam, though it experienced brief depositions, such as Salih I's initial ousting by Nafza tribesmen.[2] The state played a role in regional politics and maintained cultural significance as a hub of Arabic learning in the Rif.[3] A pivotal event was the Viking raid of 859 CE, when a fleet of 62 ships led by figures like Hastein and Björn Ironside sacked Nekor, plundering the city for eight days, enslaving inhabitants, and capturing members of the ruling family before demanding ransom.[4] The emirate's end came around 1019 CE when it was conquered by the Berber chieftain Ya'la ibn Futuh of the Azdaja tribe, leading to the fall of the Banu Salih; the region remained semi-autonomous under his successors until its destruction by Almoravid forces in 1080, with absorption into larger North African dynamics; the site's ruins are now partially submerged by the Abdelkrim Khattabi dam reservoir.[1]
History
Foundation and Early Rulers
The Emirate of Nekor was established in 710 AD by Şālih I ibn Manṣūr al-Himyari, a Yemeni Arab of Himyarite descent, who received an iqṭāʿ land grant from the Umayyad Caliphate, making it the first autonomous Sunni Maliki state in the Maghreb.[5] This foundation occurred amid the broader Arab conquests of North Africa, which had begun in 648 AD. Şālih I focused on consolidating power by converting local Berber tribes, including the Ghumārah, Şanhāja, and Nafzah, to Islam, though with mixed success as the tribes chafed under the religion's restrictions.[1] Early rule under Şālih I (710–749 AD) faced significant internal challenges, including a brief deposition around 710–749 AD by az-Zaydi of the Nafza tribe, who briefly supplanted him amid Berber discontent; Şālih was later reinstated after the tribes rejected the alternative rule.[1] Upon Şālih I's death in 749 AD, succession passed to his son al-Muʿtaṣim ibn Şālih, known for his piety, who maintained the dynasty's stability during the transition to Abbasid overlordship in the east. Al-Muʿtaṣim's reign ended with the rise of his brother Idrīs I ibn Şālih around 760 AD, who founded the city of Nekor and shifted the capital from Tamsāmān, enhancing administrative control over the Rif region. Idris I was succeeded by his son Saʿīd I ibn Idrīs (c. 760–803 AD).
Peak and External Relations
The period of Nekor's greatest prosperity, spanning the late 8th to mid-10th century, was characterized by economic growth through maritime trade and strategic diplomatic alignments, particularly with the Umayyad Caliphate of Córdoba. Under the rule of Şālih ibn Saʿīd (c. 788–816), the emirate experienced internal family disputes over succession, yet maintained a pro-Umayyad stance that ensured stability and access to Andalusian markets. His successor, Saʿīd ibn Şālih (c. 816 onward), continued this policy amid ongoing familial wars, fostering alliances that bolstered Nekor's position as a key Sunni buffer against emerging Shiʿa powers in North Africa. These rulers' alignments secured iqṭāʿ land grants from the Umayyads, enabling agricultural expansion and intermarriages with local Berber tribes, which integrated diverse populations and strengthened internal cohesion. A notable external threat during this peak came in 859, when a Viking fleet, after raiding Iberia, sailed through the Strait of Gibraltar and plundered Nekor for eight days. The Norse forces, known as Majūs in Arabic sources, captured the city, enslaved many inhabitants, and took royal captives including the daughters of the local ruler. The emirate of Córdoba intervened by ransoming the high-profile prisoners, highlighting Nekor's dependence on Umayyad support. This incursion prompted defensive architectural changes, such as fortified walls and harbor improvements, to counter future maritime raids.[4] Nekor's relations with the Umayyads deepened in the 10th century, involving naval cooperation against Fatimid expansion. The emirate provided ships and troops to Umayyad fleets combating Shiʿa incursions, while receiving military aid and economic privileges in return. Intermarriages with Berber elites further solidified these ties, promoting cultural exchange and loyalty to Sunni orthodoxy. However, Fatimid forces sacked Nekor twice, in 917 and 934, devastating the city and underscoring the emirate's vulnerability in the Sunni-Shiʿa rivalries. Concurrently, the Umayyads occupied nearby Melilla from 927 to 929 under Caliph ʿAbd al-Raḥmān III, using it as a base to support Nekor and counter Fatimid advances, thereby extending their influence over the Rif region.
Decline and Destruction
The late 10th century marked the beginning of Nekor's irreversible decline, initiated by a devastating siege around 931 by Mūsā ibn Abīʾl-Afiya, a Berber chieftain of the Miknāsa tribe aligned with the Fatimids. Mūsā's forces besieged, sacked, and burned the city of Nekor, severely weakening its defenses and economy, including the defeat and death of ruler ʿAbd al-Badīʿ ibn Şāliḥ (r. 927–929). In retaliation, the Umayyads launched a naval assault from Ceuta with a fleet of approximately forty vessels, targeting the port of al-Mazamma and further devastating the coastal infrastructure, which crippled Nekor's trade capabilities.[6] Following these assaults, the region experienced a brief period of reconstruction under later Banū Şāliḥ rulers, who maintained nominal loyalty to the Umayyad Caliphate of Córdoba while facing persistent pressures from surrounding Berber tribes, including the Ghumāra and Banū Waryaghlī, which eroded central authority through raids and territorial encroachments.[7] The Banū Şāliḥ dynasty ended in 1019, when Yaʿlā ibn al-Fatūh of the Azdaja tribe overthrew the last dynastic ruler, shifting control to local leadership amid escalating instability. This conquest marked the fall of the emirate, integrating its territories into broader North African dynamics; the site's ruins were later razed in 1080 by Almoravid leader Yūsuf ibn Tāshfīn during his campaigns in northern Morocco.[6][8]
Geography and Settlement
Location and Physical Features
Nekor was situated in the Rif Mountains of northern Morocco, a rugged coastal range extending along the Mediterranean Sea from the Strait of Gibraltar eastward toward the Moulouya River. The kingdom's core territory encompassed the lands of Berber tribes such as the Zouagha and Djeraoua, with its capital, Madinat al-Nakur, established in an alluvial valley amid the mountainous terrain. This positioning, near the modern city of al-Hoceima, placed Nekor approximately 20 kilometers inland from the coast, leveraging the natural barrier of steep slopes rising from the sea while maintaining access to maritime influences. The ruins of the site are now partially submerged by the Abdelkrim Khattabi dam reservoir.[9][10][1] The physical landscape of Nekor combined elevated highlands with fertile lowlands formed by river valleys, including the Oued Nekor, which drained into the Mediterranean and supported alluvial soils ideal for early agriculture. Surrounding coastal plains extended from the mountains to the shore, dotted with natural harbors that facilitated settlement and exchange. Key ports in the vicinity, such as Badis (Melilla area), al-Mazamma, and others like Malila and Grawa, provided essential outlets to the Alboran Sea, enabling Nekor to integrate into intercontinental trade networks linking North Africa, Iberia, and beyond. The mixed topography of peaks, rivers, and plains not only shaped defensive strategies but also channeled overland paths connecting the Rif to interior hubs like Fes and southern caravan routes toward Sijilmāsa.[10] The region's Mediterranean climate, characterized by mild winters and hot, dry summers punctuated by intense seasonal rainfall, fostered a landscape rich in biodiversity and productive for Berber agrarian practices. Annual precipitation, concentrated in autumn and winter, nourished orchards, gardens, and terraced fields in the valleys, while coastal proximity supported fisheries exploited by local tribes for sustenance and trade. Natural resources included timber from oak and pine forests on mountain slopes, grazing lands for livestock, and freshwater from rivers and springs, all of which underpinned Nekor's development as a resilient settlement amid the Rif's fragile ecosystems. This environmental setting influenced the kingdom's relocation of its capital from Tamsāmān to Nekor around 760 CE, optimizing access to fertile terrain and coastal trade.[11][10]
Urban Development and Infrastructure
The Emirate of Nekor initially established its capital at the coastal site of Tamsāmān around 710 CE, serving as a port settlement that facilitated early maritime connections before transitioning to an inland location at Madīnat al-Nakūr by approximately 760 CE, where it remained until the emirate's fall in 1019 CE.[12] This inland city, founded on the left bank of the Wādī al-Nakūr, integrated surrounding tribal territories and featured an urban layout with souks, shops, and markets organized around key gates, including Bāb al-Musallā (linked to an open-air oratory or muṣallā) and others oriented toward major access routes.[12] The settlement included gardens, orchards, and mills supported by extensive irrigation networks drawn from the river, alongside public baths (possibly a hammam) and artisanal zones for activities like ceramics production and metallurgy, reflecting a planned periurban economy.[12] At its core stood a large hypostyle mosque constructed with wooden columns of thuya wood, mud-brick walls, a tiled roof, and a beaten earth floor, emblematic of early Islamic architectural adaptation to local materials.[12] Following the devastating Viking raid of 859 CE, which exposed vulnerabilities in the coastal and inland defenses, Nekor's urban structure evolved toward greater fortification, incorporating two concentric ovoid enclosures around the central qasaba (citadel) area to encompass approximately 60 hectares, including storage silos, workshops, and a necropolis.[12] These substantial earthen ramparts, built from compacted earth (libn) rather than fired bricks, provided enclosure against invasions and marked a shift to more defensive architecture, though lacking advanced features like towers.[12] Archaeological surveys conducted in the 1980s as part of broader Moroccan-Italian regional prospections identified remnants of these walls and associated structures, revealing early Islamic construction techniques such as mud-brick and wooden framing that paralleled modest methods in Ifrīqiyā (modern Tunisia) and early Idrīsid sites, while hydraulic systems echoed Andalusian influences from al-Andalus.[12] Ceramic evidence from these surveys, including local hand-built vessels and imported glazed wares from Córdoba and Bajjana, underscored Nekor's integration into Mediterranean trade circuits, with forms and techniques linking it culturally to both Ifrīqiyā and al-Andalus without indicating large-scale production.[12] As the earliest documented planned Islamic urban center in the western Maghreb, predating sites like Fès, Nekor exemplified fortified medieval settlement patterns, blending tribal federation with enclosed defenses to sustain autonomy amid regional pressures until its incorporation into larger polities in 1019 CE.[12]
Governance and Economy
Political Structure and Administration
The Emirate of Nekor was governed by the Banū Şālih dynasty, which claimed descent from Himyarite Arabs of Yemen but faced disputes over its origins, with some contemporary accounts suggesting links to the indigenous Nafzī Berbers of the Rif region.[13] The dynasty's founder, Şālih ibn Manşūr, received the territory as an iqṭāʿ—a form of tax-farming land grant—from the Umayyad Caliph al-Walīd I around 710 CE in recognition of his role in pacifying and converting local Berber tribes to Islam.[13] Ruling as emirs, the Banū Şālih maintained a federative political structure that integrated Arab leadership with Berber tribal hierarchies, avoiding direct tribal chieftaincy while forging alliances through personal ties and political maneuvering.[14] This system emphasized Sunni Maliki jurisprudence, with emirs leading communal prayers and promoting Qurʾanic education to unify diverse groups against neighboring Kharijite and Shiʿite influences.[13] Succession within the dynasty combined familial inheritance with elective elements influenced by Berber tribal consultations, often leading to conflicts, exiles, and interventions by groups such as the Miknāsa, Ghumāra, Şanhādja, and Nafza.[13] For instance, tribal support was pivotal in restoring claimants like Şālih ibn Saʿīd after exiles to al-Andalus, though revolts—such as those by the Banū Waryaghal and Kaznnaya—highlighted tensions over fiscal impositions.[13] The emirs acknowledged and incorporated these tribal structures into governance, delegating local authority to notables while centralizing control in the capital at Madīnat Nakūr, which featured a fortified qaṣaba, mosques, souks, and irrigation networks to support administrative oversight of surrounding territories.[13] As a client state, Nekor aligned exclusively with the Sunni Umayyad caliphate—first in Damascus and later in Córdoba—contrasting sharply with the Shiʿite Fatimids, through military aid, refuge for Umayyad exiles, and intermarital ties inferred from shared Nafzī kinship.[13] This loyalty manifested in grants of protection, such as Umayyad forces expelling Fatimid governors in 917 and 935 CE, and celebrations in Córdoba of Nekor's victories, including the dispatch of severed heads of Fatimid officials.[13] Administratively, the emirs taxed Berber coastal communities for marine resources and trade via ports like al-Mazzama, sustaining autonomy while integrating tribes through shared economic benefits from caravan routes and agriculture, though such levies occasionally sparked uprisings among marginal groups like the Miknāsa.[13]
Trade, Agriculture, and Resources
Nekor's economy was anchored in agriculture, which thrived in the fertile coastal plains and inland valleys of the Rif region, where Berber tribal practices supported cereal cultivation, livestock breeding, and fruit orchards including pomegranates and pears. These agricultural outputs, supplemented by gardens producing local crops, sustained urban markets in the capital of Nakur and reinforced its role as an entrepôt for trans-regional commerce, with surplus produce feeding both local populations and trade networks.[10][15] The emirate's trade routes positioned it as a vital hub, linking overland paths from Fes and Sijilmāsa in southern Morocco to Mediterranean ports across the Alboran Sea, facilitating the exchange of goods from the southern Rif hinterlands and al-Andalus. Nakur's port, alongside secondary facilities like al-Mazamma, handled maritime traffic that connected to Ifrīqya and Iberian markets, exporting ceramics, textiles, and agricultural products while importing luxuries such as gold and ivory from sub-Saharan routes. This network peaked in the 8th and 9th centuries, driving urban prosperity through diverse marketplaces that blended Arab, Berber, and Andalusian merchants.[10][15] Resource extraction and taxation bolstered economic independence, with Berber tribes contributing marine products like fish and coastal goods through ports such as al-Mazamma, taxed to fund the iqṭāʿ system of land grants that allocated revenues from farms and fisheries to military elites. This system integrated rural agricultural productivity with urban trade, enabling the export of ceramics and other Rif-sourced items to Ifrīqya and al-Andalus, while ensuring the emirate's self-sufficiency amid regional rivalries.[10]
Society and Culture
Population and Social Composition
The population of the Emirate of Nekor consisted primarily of indigenous Berber tribes, with dominant groups including the Ghumāra (also known as Ghomara) and Şanhādja (Sanhaja) in the Rif Mountains region, alongside Zenata confederations such as the Nafza and Miknāsa tribes that bordered or influenced the area.[16] These Berber communities formed the social and economic backbone, organized around tribal loyalties that emphasized kinship, pastoralism, and local governance structures. The ruling Banū Şālih dynasty introduced an Arab element of Himyarite descent from Yemen, established by founder Ṣāliḥ ibn Manṣūr al-Ḥimyarī in 710 CE through a Caliphal grant; this Arab elite settled post-conquest and integrated with Berber society, influencing customs while relying on tribal alliances for stability.[1] Amid occasional revolts, such as the brief elevation of a Nafza tribesman, az-Zaydi, as leader around 749 CE, these alliances helped consolidate power.[1] Coastal urban communities in Nekor's ports, such as those near modern Melilla, blended Berber fishers with Arab traders and later Andalusi immigrants (including Mozarabs fleeing al-Andalus), supporting multilingual markets oriented toward Mediterranean commerce with Iberia and the eastern Caliphate.[16] This ethnic mixing strengthened the emirate's trade networks but was underpinned by persistent Berber tribal loyalties, which often challenged Arab dominance until the dynasty's end in 1019 CE.[1]
Religion and Cultural Influences
The Emirate of Nekor adhered exclusively to Sunni Maliki Islam, a doctrinal choice that unified its diverse Arab and Berber populations against the expansionist Shiʿa Fatimid Caliphate in the 10th century. This commitment to the Maliki school of jurisprudence, one of the four major Sunni traditions, emphasized a moderate interpretation of Islamic law that resonated with local customs and facilitated social cohesion amid regional sectarian tensions. By prioritizing Sunni orthodoxy, Nekor's rulers positioned their polity as a bulwark of traditional Islam in the western Maghreb, influencing subsequent states in the adoption of Maliki fiqh as the dominant legal framework. Founder Şālih I ibn Mansur played a pivotal role in the Islamization of the Rif Berber tribes, initiating conversions shortly after establishing the emirate in 710 CE under Umayyad auspices. Although early apostasy among some tribes led to resistance against Islamic restrictions, Şālih I's efforts, bolstered by caliphal military support, resulted in re-conversions and gradual integration of Islam into tribal life. These conversions not only solidified Nekor's religious identity but also linked it to broader Umayyad networks, setting a precedent for Sunni adherence in Berber societies. This process briefly intersected with the social composition of the population, where tribal structures adapted to Islamic norms without fully erasing Berber kinship systems.[1] Nekor's urban culture exemplified a synthesis of Arab and Berber elements, evident in the bilingual use of Arabic and Tamazight (Berber languages) in daily administration and trade, fostering a hybrid identity unique to the Rif region. Key religious sites, including the grand mosque of Nekor and associated oratories, served as communal hubs that reinforced Maliki practices and early Islamic traditions in the western Maghreb. These structures hosted Friday prayers, legal deliberations, and educational sessions on fiqh, embodying the emirate's role in disseminating Sunni Islam westward and nurturing a shared religious ethos among inhabitants.
Legacy
Archaeological Significance
Archaeological investigations at Nekor, the capital of the 8th–11th century Salihid emirate in northern Morocco's Rif region, have been limited but revealing, primarily through surveys and sondages conducted since the 1980s. Initial regional prospections in the mid-1980s by Patrice Cressier integrated Nekor into a broader study of the emirate's territory, identifying surface traces of medieval occupation amid environmental changes caused by modern infrastructure. More targeted efforts in 1996–1997, as part of a Franco-Moroccan program led by Patrice Cressier, M. Acién Almansa, and L. Erbati, involved topographic mapping, aerial photo analysis, and three limited test excavations (sondages A, B, and C).[17] These uncovered evidence of urban structures, including a hypostyle building on the site's summit interpreted as a congregational mosque, constructed with raw bricks (libn) and wooden thuya columns, aligning with 11th-century textual descriptions by al-Bakri of a mosque resembling that of Alexandria.[17] The excavations highlighted Nekor's role as the earliest known medieval Islamic urban center in the western Maghreb, with planned layouts featuring two concentric earthen enclosures: an inner circular wall enclosing the core settlement (qaṣaba and mosque) and an outer ovoid perimeter spanning approximately 60 hectares, incorporating necropolises, silos, and artisanal zones.[17] Sondage C on the inner wall confirmed construction in compacted earth without towers or gates identified, suggesting a defensive system adapted to the rugged Rif topography rather than Abbasid-inspired models, with possible expansions following invasions by the Umayyads and Banu Midrar in the 8th–9th centuries. Artifacts included local unglazed wheel-thrown pottery (e.g., marmites and casseroles) produced from nearby clays, alongside imported glazed ceramics: green and manganese varieties from Cordoba and Madinat al-Zahra in al-Andalus, brown-glazed types from Bajjana (Pechina), and potential examples from central Maghreb or Ifrīqiya featuring ombilical bases.[17] These finds underscore Nekor's function as a key 9th–10th century trade hub on Mediterranean routes, relaying goods between al-Andalus, sub-Saharan networks via Sijilmassa, and eastern Islamic centers, contrasting with the more elaborate stone architecture of later Almoravid sites like Fès. Preservation challenges persist due to the site's proximity to modern Bni Bouayach, where 1980s construction of the Abd el-Krim el-Khattabi dam flooded upstream areas, buried ancient vestiges under a worker settlement, and altered irrigation systems, leading to erosion and agricultural encroachment. Earlier American sondages in the 1970s by Charles Redman yielded minimal results, such as a possible silo, highlighting the difficulties of working on a disturbed urban mound. Ongoing digs remain scarce, with recent Maroco-Italian prospections focusing on peripheral ports like al-Mazamma providing supplementary data on trade but not core urban layers; urgent calls for comprehensive excavations emphasize the risk of further heritage loss before irreversible damage.
Historical Impact on the Region
Nekor pioneered the model of an autonomous Sunni state in the Maghreb, establishing a governance structure that emphasized Maliki jurisprudence and local Berber tribal integration, which set precedents for later Berber dynasties such as the Almoravids.[10] This early framework of Sunni orthodoxy, distinct from Shi'a influences elsewhere, influenced the religious and administrative adherence of subsequent regimes in northern Morocco, promoting a hybrid Berber-Arab political identity that persisted through dynastic transitions.[18] The emirate played a pivotal role in the Islamization of the Rif region, with its rulers credited in medieval chronicles for converting local Berber tribes and establishing Islamic institutions like mosques and markets in urban centers such as Nakur. Al-Bakrī's accounts highlight Nekor's efforts in spreading Sunni Islam among the Nafza and Sanhaja Berbers, while resisting Fatimid Shi'a expansion through defensive alliances and territorial control in the 10th century.[10] This process solidified the Rif's incorporation into Dar al-Islam, fostering cultural and religious cohesion that outlasted the emirate itself. Nekor's trade networks significantly shaped the economy of northern Morocco, with its ports like Badis and al-Mazamma serving as key entrepôts for Mediterranean and trans-Saharan commerce, linking sub-Saharan goods to al-Andalus and Europe. Al-Bakrī describes these Rif ports as bustling hubs for textiles, metals, and agricultural products, laying the groundwork for the region's enduring coastal trade orientation that evolved into modern economic centers.[10] Following its conquest in 1019, Nekor integrated into successive empires from the Almoravids to the Marinids, preserving a Berber-Arab hybrid identity through continued Maliki practices and tribal structures in the Rif. This seamless absorption ensured the region's political and cultural continuity, influencing the broader Moroccan state's formation without disrupting local autonomies.[10]
